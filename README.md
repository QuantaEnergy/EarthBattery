I've been working on this for decades. Let me give you some details. - There are some things that will help and hurt the probes.  Too much moisture, or a complete absence of moisture, the right amount is what you will find under the canopy of most large trees. This is called their drip line, where dew and rain (etc.) fall and become shaded at least half of the time. The other thing is stones. If your probe is next to a stone it will dampen and also cause more corrosion on the probe. I use a 3" powered auger to make a 24" hole, extract any stones and then pack this back in around the probe.

The stringing them in series works, but the amperage remains very low. The distance between them is also critical. Too close and they simply don't work, and beyond 30" you get inconsistent power. I'm an electrical engineer and have created coils and a small transistor and capacitor circuit I seal at the top of the probes (I call them antennas because you can use these in ground and high in the air to gain the same effect) to gather and channel the power out of a grid of prunes.  The most I work with is 50 Volts and 15 Amps per half acre. That is equal to a few modest solar panels and will charge batteries 24/7 with a solar and wind controller to batteries and a house inverter.
